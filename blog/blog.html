Nov 9 2018 - Design Revisited

Our Design Approach 2.0

The Kiwi Design System

Over the past two weeks we have a design team have been doing a lot of introspection hoping to gain some insight into how we can do a better job empowering the growing development team to create an intuitive, visually pleasing and consistent user experience across all of our products. The result of this exercise was a renewed commitment to a strategy we admittedly did not do a great job implementing after the completion of the initial Advantage Command Center Launch – creating a modular design library which we called Project Kiwi.

While I believe that Kiwi has had some success at improving handoffs and design awareness, it has failed to create a consistent single source of truth that explicitly lays out our design philosophy. To that end, I have proposed that we renew are Kiwi efforts and create a Design System. I Design System goes beyond what a design library is as it becomes its own project within an organization. Well done Design Systems are centralized, strictly followed and comprehensive enough that developers, designers and quality assurance professionals can all rely on it to remove the vast majority of the design ambiguity they encounter.

Design Systems have become the standard as we moved into the next generation of the web and it is very common for companies to make their Design Systems publicly available. If you are interested you can find a nice repository of design systems on Adele at: Adele.com.

After speaking with Tim, Michael and Thomas, I put together a proposal for the Design System I would like to create for our organization. You can find the proposal I pitched to Richard, Shelly and Matt at: Design System Proposal. I am happy to say that as a design team, this is now the direction that we will be heading in and that we are already working on the practical implications of this proposal.

Oct 26 2018 - UX Takes a Vacation

What Did Design Do On Vacation?

A Trip to the Paris Museum National D'Histoire Naturelle

While we were on vacation we took our dinosaur loving three year old to a museum which had a special exhibit all about the only dinosaur that really matters to him, the Tyrannosaurus-Rex. I expected to enter the exhibit and be instantly overwhelmed by boredom as we shuffled through a stuffy exhibit full of academic signs and roped off glass cases. However, my expectation as a user could not have been more wrong. What we experienced instead was a collection of exhibits whose focus on Natural Mapping, Tone and effective Mental Models made for an amazingly fun and interactive visit. As a result I thought it might be fun to go through the UX of the exhibit that in my opinion made it so successful.

T-Rex Bike Survival

Ever wonder how you would fare if you were being chased by a hungry Tyrannosaurus-Rex? (which Hollywood leaves me to wonder if they were ever not hungry?). One the exhibits we used let Finn and I find out. The exhibit designers cleverly mapped my real world experience of riding a bike to the virtual one they created by positioning a stationary bike between two screens. They then met my expected mental model by showing what was in front of me, behind me, in my mirrors, how far away the T-Rex was and how close the finish line was. All together it created an very fun interaction which I can proudly say Finn and I survived.

What if a T-Rex ate you?

This might have been one of the more morbid exhibits, but the natural mapping done here was great. The goal of the exhibit was to see how long a T-Rex could live if they ate you, or you and your family and friends. The scale was huge, which easily suggested that I could get on it with the entire family. Second the numbers going up mapped easily to the number of days the T-Rex would be full and I guess happy after eating me. In this case, I don't think the T-Rex would be all to happy for that long...

Paint a T-Rex

According to the science folks at the Paris Natural History museum we have no idea what color scheme the T-Rex choose for their UI. Therefore, the French thought it would be fun to let museum goers create their own versions of what a T-Rex might have looked like using a projector screen to colorize and accessorize a T-Rex. All of that is nothing special, but what is special is how the decided to incorporate Natural Mapping into the controls for the interaction.

Instead of just pointing to a color and then rubbing the color on a stationary dinosaur, the designers opted to let the user paint with a spray paint can a "restless" T-Rex who moves around and reacts to what the user is doing. Allowing the dinosaur to move around and adding different sound effects for each type of spray paint manufacturers a unique tone that invites the user to continue to interact with the exhibit in order to discover what else they can do.

Exhibit Placeholders

Another cool approach to setting the tone of the exhibit was how the designers created video placeholders for each of the exhibits that had short audio/video clips of scientists explaining what the user what looking at. Instead of just having a simple " Press the button below to view this stereotypical boring academic lecture " they recorded the scientist who would be presenting the information just hanging out waiting for someone to come by and interact with them. The result is a room full of relatable looking people waiting around, checking their phones, looking at the ceiling, eating a snack, etc. This creates a much more relatable tone since if I was stuck all day in a museum waiting to talk to be people to, that is what I would be doing as well.

Projection Book

The last exhibit in the hall was a large book that depicted what the scientists knew about the life of the T-Rex they had on display. How they choose to display that information once again shows their attention to natural mapping, tone and mental models. What they did was create a picture book of the T-Rex's life, which was an awesome choice, kids and parents alike love books. Second what they did was reach their intended persona's love of interaction make the book have lots of animations and then project it onto an actual physical set of pages kids could turn.

New/Interesting UX Fact (Craig)

One question or topic that we frequently address as Designers is how to create a usable and efficient data entry form. I think a lot of the time we as humans look at a form and want to put it in columns because it feels balanced and as though it is a great use of space. Multiple Column Layouts also track well with other experiences that we have: columned newspapers, articles, instruction manuals, etc. However in reality UX Researchers (Yup, that is actually a thing) have found through various studies that the fastest form to fill out and the easiest to comprehend is a single column layout with one form description to one form input. It might feel like a waste of space, but laying out the form in a single column greatly reduces the possibility of user error as the single column layout makes it very clear which entry the user should move to next. For way more information than you probably want to know about this topic feel free to check out the latest study released by the Baymard Institute at: Form Field Usability: Avoid Multi-Column Layouts (13% Get it Wrong)

Color Range Design for MicroSites - Meg

While converting our Sales Microsite Mockups to High Fidelity we realized that we were going to need a way of dynamically assigning a range of colors to an unknown number of widgets which would only be determined at runtime. We didn’t want to create a bunch of constants, so Meg set out to come up with a more elegant solution.

Meg considered several different options, including variations of tone, opacity and hue. However she ended up inventing a far better solution with...analogous colors! (I know try to contain your color wheels!). Her solution was to take a range of analogous colors that were &qout;On-Brand&qout; and divide that range by the number of elements that we will need to color, such as bars in a bar graph or steps in a flow. This solution is easy to calculate as all the developer needs to do is change one number in the rgb code by the step amount. In addition this solution means that our range will always have the same difference between the start and the finish (since we always use the same start and finish rgb codes), which will be visually pleasing to the user. New/Interesting UX Fact (Craig)

Beauty vs. Usability. There is a fine line here. As UX designers we try our best to make things beautiful, but often we risk getting pulled in too far to the point we the product suffers. It is a struggle far older than the web, for example at one point in time, this was a highly sought after product because it was so beautiful.

One example that I ran across lately of this struggle was a really awesome site for a museum that lets you control experience you see on your computer screen by swiping your phone. The way it is set up is really quite neat. Your phone turns into the map of the museum and your computer screen is your virtual view into the exhibits. However, after using it a bit you quickly find out just how difficult it is to actually manage the two different experiences at once. It is just one designer's opinion, but I think this one falls to heavy on the beauty side at the cost of the usability side (especially when you consider the accessibility of the experience). If you want to try it out yourself visit: Okean Elzy, follow the instructions on screen once it loads to pair your phone and your off to races!

New/Interesting UX Fact (Craig)

Abstracting/Reframing is a brainstorming tool that Meg and I both use a lot when we are developing designs for vMOX and Advantage. The general idea behind the approach is to stop trying to solve the specific design problem you are facing and instead consider solving the larger problem it represents. For example a lot of the time we will abstract a problem down to "well this is just a categized table" or this is "just some form a newsfeed". When we make that abstraction we then free ourselves to look for inspiration in examples that are not directly related to the types of designs that we create here at vMOX/Advantage. It may sound silly, but so far we have found it to be a great way to come up with some really interesting "Design B's". By the way, Design B, would be an awesome name for a blog!

New/Interesting UX Fact (Craig)

Lets talk about Affordances. An Affordance descibles all actions that are made phiscally possible by the properties of the object itself (or an environment). For example I bet at least some you tried to click on the word Affordance a couple of sentences back. Why did you do that? Most likely you saw a blue underlined word and knew from your previous experiences that you should be able to click on it. However, in that case you were lied to and it was a dead link (how frustrating right?). Other examples that might come to mind which you might have come across in the physical world include door handles that suggest you should pull a door, or bottle caps which suggest you should twist them.

Another Example - Light Switch Design

A recent pleasant encounter I had with well implemented Affordances is with my new pair of running shoes. They have these great laces where all you have to do is push a tab in order to lace them, however then you are left with a large amount lace with nothing to do with it. Naturally I went to go tuck that extra lace back under the lace itself only to find this tab on the tongue of the shoe waiting for me. It was designed with a loop on the top suggesting that I should pull up on it, which then created a space for me to put my extra lace! Come on people, lets celebrate the little victories in life.

My Long Leftover Lace
The Affordance in Action

Affordances are extremely powerful. When they are correctly implemented they can effortlessly guide the user through your interface. However when they are implemented incorrectly as with the lead link I introduced in the opening paragraph of this section, they can drive a user nuts. This process of driving the user nuts with poorly implemented affordances is known as the difference between Perceived Affordance, what we think a object or element does and Actual Affordance, which is what the object is really capable of doing.

New/Interesting UX Fact (Craig)

So why do we still need UX? With so many design friendly tools out there in the world, including Wordpress, Countless Online Templates, Well Documented best practices, plus all of the successful user experiences which have already been developed that we can look at from companies such as Air BnB, Netflix and Amazon, why in the world does anyone ever need a UX Professional on their team anymore? This question comes up a lot and I hear it frequently, especially when I first start working for a new company or client: Why can't I just take Amazon's approach and copy on my site? It is successful for them, why shouldn't be successful for me?

The truth is, you could, but it won't work. It won't work because you are not Amazon. Your site, no matter how similar it might be to Amazon does not serve the exact same purpose for the same user base as Amazon does and so it will never be as successful as you would wish it to be. OnePortal User Management - Craig

Late last week we found out that some of our customers had to reach our to our Account Managers each time they created an employee in OnePortal in order to manage that new user's security group. Additionally our employee management process did not take the advantage of an opportunity to inform our user's about the different secruity options available for new users. So who started this User Experience deal anyway? The actual concept of UX can be traced back as far as the 1400's with Leonardo Da Vinci's "Kitchen Nightmare"(hilarious story about an out of control conveyor belt driven kitchen complete with a fake fire alarm scare). However the first time the term User Experience actually came up as a job title was when a electrical engineer and cognitive scientist by the name of Don Norman joined Apple asked to be called a "User Experience Architect". From this postion he eventually became the Vice President of the Advanced Technology Group where he lead Design effort which helped to bring about the successful launches of new Apple product design experiences.

Today Don Norman is one of the founders of the leading agency for Usability Research, Consulting and training, the Nielsen Norman Group. He is also the director of the Design Lab at the University of California, San Diego. So in summary Don Norman was basically the the first UX Professional - remind you of anyone else?

The use of Microinteractions has increased greatly in the web as the capabilities of modern frameworks have recognized the importance of creating truly polished products.

So is it clear as mud what exactly they are? To be honest it took me a long time to get the hang of it, as there is a very fine line you have to walk with Microinteractions, as it is easy to get caught in the trap of "too much of a good thing". New/Interesting UX Fact (Craig)

Did you know there is actually a name for the UX process that Meg and I follow (and that there is more than one UX process to begin with?)? The process that we have had the most success with is known as the Double Diamond Approach, which also sometimes referred to as the Divergent/Convergent Design Thinking Approach. The other day I ran across one of the best (if not possibly one of the most humorous) diagrams describing this process:

Double Diamond UX Process

So what makes this approach better than all the others? In my opinion this approach is tailor made for one or two person UX teams as it forces the UX Design to shy away from becoming too attached to one particular solution to a design challenge. In a nutshell this approach suggests that the best way to come up with creative and useful solutions is to first think of every possible way you can to solve the problem (divergent thinking). This is often referred to as Rapid Ideation and is quickly followed up by a feedback session where the designer gets input from the product owner(s) on the multiple directions they have come up with. An example of this is when we decided as a team to move to a ridged microsite navigation approach for the backend applications.

The hope is that by this point all of the stakeholders have reached a consensus on HOW we are going to the solve the challenge and we can start working on what that solution is going to look like. This is where we get to start coming up with all sorts of crazy design approaches that will fit our solution approach and solve the challenge. It is at this point were you see us put out a lot of A/B Designs that look wildly different from one another. This is a common tactic that we use to help us converge again on the design which is going to best fit the solution we need to make an interface for. Going back to the example of the navigation design you hopefully saw me putting out three different A/B tests in order to help me converge on the design direction that would best fit our user's needs.

Once it feels like we have converged on a design direction we begin iterating on that direction until the design gets the sign off from all the stakeholders (hint that is you, and yes, I mean all of you). This is the point of the process when you will see us create multiple folders in the Axure file each named "Iteration X". The goal of all of these iterations is to finalize the details of the design in order to get it ready for handoff. Going back to our example of the navigation redesign, this is when you will see the navigation shift from white to black based on the feedback from leadership.

New/Interesting UX Fact (Craig)

Did you know that UX itself is not a role? Just like "Development " isn’t a role, but rather a type of position, UX can refer to a wide collection of different positions, each with their own specific meanings. The most common of these roles are:

  • UX Researcher: focus on unpacking challenges through the use of studies, interviews and analysis. Their work generally results in the creation of StoryMaps, Personas, Journey Maps and more
  • Information Architects: focus on how to organize content within a site and how the overall experience should flow. Their work generally results in the creation of Low Fidelity Mockups, Flow Diagrams and more
  • Interaction Designer: focus on tasks such as what screens should go where in an applications, how different actions within a screen will effect the content, and transitions between screens. Their work generally results in the creation of High Fedility Mockups
  • Visual Designer: focus on typography, layout, color, graphics and texture. Their work generally results in the creation of Icon Packs, Style Guides and High Fidelity Mockups

The idea here is that if we can develop and release smaller bits of complete functionality and get feedback on it we can utilize that feedback to guide the decisions we make regarding what gets designed and developed next.

The MVP version of the Communications Management mockup will allow the user to see the status of each of their reports, view an example of a report and approve/deny that report for release. You can view a mockup of the new Communications Management MVP at: Communications Management MVP Mockup.

New/Interesting UX Fact (Meg)

Since I have color on the brain this week, here are interesting UX facts about color. Did you know that Google once tested 41 shades of blue for their links to find out which color people were most likely to click on? You can read more about how to use color when designing here: Intro to Color Theory and here: Tips for choosing colors.

Also, did you know that Mark Zuckerberg is red-green colorblind? He chose blue for Facebook's primary color because it is the color that he can see best. 8% of men are red-green color blind and it is an important factor to consider when designing. You can simulate different kinds of color blindness using ColorOracle; color contrast checkers can also be very helpful.
